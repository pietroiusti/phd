The Quasi-Persuasive Nature of Disagreement

Giulio Pietroiusti

What is the nature of morality and value?

What's the difference between “science” and “ethics”? Between “description” and “evaluation”?

This is one the greatest and oldest problems in philosophy

Why?

The evaluative domain seems to involve an element of subjectivity.

Think, in particular, of matters of “taste”.

Values are often not fully perceived as “facts”.

Do we deem things to be good, because we approve of them?

Or do we approve of them, because they are good?

Questions like this one have been asked since the dawn of philosophy.

Most major philosophers have dealt with the nature of value.

“Analytic” philosophy:

What are ethical properties? (metaphysics)

What is the meaning of ethical terms? (philosophy of language)

What kind of mental states are ethical beliefs?(philosophy of mind)

Disagreement

The phenomenon and concept of disagreement has had a special place in the meta-evaluative investigation.

Sextus Empiricus...

In analytic philosophy, since G.E. Moore (1912), issues about disagreement and value have been debated.

A problem with disagreement

Moore argued that subjectivism was at odds with common sense.

Subjectivism was an attempt to account for the non-objective aspect of morality.

Subjectivism: “x is wrong” ≈ “x has property of being approved by me”

If subjectivism were right...

  • A: x is wrong
  • B: No, x is not wrong

↓

  • C: x has the property of being disapproved by me
  • D: No [?!], x does not have the property of being disapproved by me.

A and B disagree. But: C and D do not.

Moore: there must be something wrong with subjectivism. Goodness must be a non-natural property.

Charles L. Stevenson

I have particularly engaged with the thought of Charles Stevenson

Stevenson gave an answer to Moore.

He offered a metaethical picture according to which the difference between description and evaluation is to be found at the psychological level.

Famously, he distinguished “Disagreement in belief” vs “Disagreement in attitude”.

My engagement with Stevenson's ideas has led to the developement of several projects.

History of metaethical noncognitivism

A noncognitivist picture of ethics

A minimalist, or deflationary, understanding of truth (and related notions like belief and fact)

An account of disagreement

Experimental research

Some of the upshots of those investigations have been published and constitute the content of doctoral thesis

The Challenging Element

One element that keeps the papers connected:

“a motive for altering or calling into question the attitude of the other”.

The papers

The first paper has a quite narrow focus: it is concerned with whether moral and taste judgments are different with the respect to the presence of disposition to challenge conflicting judgments.

The second paper is a much more ambitious project: there I attempt to provide an account of what it is for two people to have a disagreement.

The First Paper

Disagreement and Conflict: How Moral and Taste Judge- ments Do Not Differ

Challenging in Eriksson

Eriksson thinks that the challenging element can be used to distinguish moral judgments and taste judgments.

The intuitive asymmetry

Eriksson aims to explain the following (alleged) intuitive asymmetry:

Intuitive Asymmetry: Conflicting moral judgments are intuititively taken as mistaken, whereas conflicting taste judgments typically are not.

Explaining the intuitive asymmetry

In order to explain this asymmetry (I won't go into the details), Eriksson puts forward the following claim:

Moral judgments are necessarily attended by a disposition to challenge conflicting judgments, whereas taste judgments are not.

Eriksson's arguments

His arguments are of two types:

First, arguments in support of the thesis that taste judgments are only contingently connected with a challenging disposition.

Second, arguments to the effect that moral judgments are necessarily attended by a challenging disposition.

My Contribution

The contribution of the paper consists in showing that none of Eriksson's arguments discloses a substantial difference with respect to the presence of a disposition to challenge conflicting judgments.

The question of whether moral judgments and taste judgments are different with respect to the presence of a challenging disposition remains open.

The Second Paper

Having a disagreement: expression, persuasion and demand

In contrast with the narrowly focused character of the first paper, in the second paper I embark in a much more ambitious project.

My aim is to put forward a new account of what it is to have a disagreement.

State VS Activity

It is common to distinguish two ordinary senses of ‘disagreement’:

  • On the one hand, disagreement can be a kind of state people are in (being in disagreement). Disagreement in the state sense requires no interaction.

  • On the other hand, disagreement can be a type of linguistic activity speakers engage in (having a disagreement). Disagreement as an activity is a type of interaction.

Having a disagreement?


“Whether [two persons] are having a disagreement [. . . ] depends only on their attitudes and actions towards each other.” (MacFarlane 2014)

Having a disagreement?

But what are the “attitudes and actions” that constitute the activity of having a disagreement?

What kind of interaction is the activity of having a disagreement?

Expression and Persuasion

I present and rejects two views:

EXPRESSION: A and B are having a disagreement iff they are expressing conflicting attitudes (See Plunkett&Sundell 2013, 2021; Zeman 2020)

PERSUASION: A and B are having a disagreement iff they are expressing conflicting attitudes and at least one of them is attempting to change the attitude of the other.(Sinclair 2021)

Presented as a Stevensonian view. Inspired by Stevenson's challenging element.

My criticism

EXPRESSION is too little. PERSUASION is too much.

EXPRESSION is not sufficient.

PERSUASION is not necessary.

The Solution

A view that goes beyond EXPRESSION but that does not go as far as PERSUASION goes

Demand

DEMAND: A and B are having a disagreement iff they are expressing conflicting attitudes and at least one of them is demanding that her attitude be shared by the other.


In my usage, demanding consists in advancing the normative claim that the other should, or is required, to agree.


DEMAND is a Stevensonian view as well (a development a of the “calling into question element”).

Future research

Disagreement in the state sense

A question which I have only indirectly addressed: What is disagreement in the state sense?

It's unclear what the relation between the state and the activity is.

There is no convergence in the literature.

In particular: is there such a thing as a non-doxastic disagreement (disagreement without conflicting beliefs)?

Many have answered this question affirmatively, in different ways.

Experimental Philosophy

Experimental research on disagreement and values has been growing

  • Folk moral objectivism (Sarkissian et al. 2011)
  • Moral semantics (Khoo & Knobe 2016)
  • The nature of aesthetic judgments (Cova et al. 2019)

However the issue of non-doxastic disagreement has not been directly addressed. (One exception: Shemmer & Bex-Priestley 2021)

My Experimental investigation

I have conducted some experiments to shed light on whether there is such a thing as non-doxastic disagreement.

This is unpublished work so it could not have been included in the thesis.

The study, which was pre-registrered on AsPredicted and asks whether people are inclined to ascribe disagreement in cases of conflicting beliefs (e.g. A believes that the cake is tasty and B doesn't) to a greater extent than they are in cases of analogous conflicting non-doxastic mental states (e.g. A likes the cake and B doesn't).

408 people were recruited through Prolific. Participants were excluded from the final sample if they failed to complete the survey (n = 5) or provided an incorrect answer to a comprehension check (n = 56). The final sample included 347 participants.

Participants were randomly assigned to one of eight conditions (four doxastic conditions and four non-doxastic)

In each condition participants received a vignette describing two persons who live in different countries, don't know each other, and hold two ``conflicting'' mental states.

Participants were asked how much they agreed (on a scale from 1 to 7) with the statement that the two persons described in the vignette disagree.

Data were analyzed using a linear mixed effects model, with state-type as a fixed effect and domain-type as a random effect (with random intercepts but not random slopes).

There was a significant effect such that participants agreed more with the statement in the doxastic conditions than in the non-doxastic conditions, χ2(1) = 54.29, p < .001.
